Computing Without Computers

Seminar Abstract

 

Ian Page

Business Development Director, Seven Spires Investments Ltd.

Visiting Professor, Imperial College London

Founder, Celoxica Ltd. – the Handel-C Company

 

 

The general-purpose computer, and the microprocessor in particular, has changed radically the world we live in. We now have fifty years of experience of computing using it, but I believe that there is now a second, emerging model for computing. The two have very different cost-performance characteristics, so the new model will both complement the standard way of implementing software using a computer, and even displace it in certain areas.

 

We consider the two separate innovations of Hardware Compilation, and Reconfigurable Logic (such as the FPGA or Field-Programmable Gate Array). Taken together, I believe they are a new technological phenomenon that will prove able to alter, or even reverse, some of the pressures that have shaped today’s computing systems. 

 

FPGA chips allow us to build hardware in milliseconds under software control, and hardware compilation allows us to turn programs into hardware automatically. Together, they allow programs to be turned into application-specific circuits rather than machine code running on a processor.  Applications implemented in this way can sometimes execute much faster since there is no sequential computer around to slow down the computation. Programmers can implement these applications-in-hardware, sometimes in mere hours, working only at the level of parallel, real-time programs expressed in a nearly familiar programming language. This is in marked contrast with the past, where an implementation in hardware would require specialist electronic designers and a very expensive and lengthy development program.

 

I will outline the research work I initiated in 1990 at Oxford University to address the opportunity of compiling programs into hardware and the results to date of exploring where the limits might lie when computing is done without general-purpose computers. I will review the ongoing commercial exploitation of this work in the form of the Handel-C programming language and show how far we have progressed in being able to map the same application program either into hardware, or software, or a combination of both optimised for the particular application.

 

The talk will include live demonstrations of hardware systems generated by programmers that show many of the advantages of designing and building systems this way. I will also review some of the startling advances made recently by Celoxica customers using this new design and implementation technology.

 

 

 

The talk will be illustrated by demonstrations and it will be necessary to have available a VGA video projector or large monitor for a PowerPoint presentation and for the real-time demonstration of (video-generating) compiled hardware.
