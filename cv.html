Summary

Originally from Rochdale in Lancashire, I studied Electrical Engineering at King's College, London, followed by a Master's degree in Systems Engineering at City University.  I worked for five years in the computing industry and moved into academia in 1976 at London University. My research interests have always straddled the interface between hardware and software, particularly in the areas of parallel hardware and computer graphics.  I joined Oxford University Computing Laboratory in 1984 and started a new research theme in 1990 on Hardware Compilation and was promoted to Reader in Computation.

 

I left Oxford University in 1999 to join Celoxica Ltd, the company I had founded with two others to exploit the results of my research effort to develop practical and automatic methods of turning software programs into effective parallel hardware implementations. Growing rapidly from three people in early 1998, the company now has around ninety employees in Oxford, California, Japan and Singapore. I have helped the company raise over $60m of investment to develop the Handel-C language and bring it to market. Since launching the product in March 2001, the company has won numerous awards and customer testimonials – see www.celoxica.com for details.

 

Currently I work as an independent consultant (with about 25% of my time devoted to Celoxica) and as Visiting Professor in the Department of Computing at Imperial College. I have subsidiary interests in early-stage technology companies and am a founder member of Oxford Investors Club.

 

Research Interests

Since 1976, my research interests have centred on implementing software algorithms as high-performance hardware, and graphics algorithms in particular. To this end, I have been significantly involved in many different activities including parallel programming, compiler implementation, parallel algorithms, computer architecture, multi-paradigm parallel processors, VLSI design, CAD and EDA software, and most recently hardware compilation. I have a history of successful bidding for academic research grants in a number of research areas. Even without adjustment for inflation over the last 25 years, these grants have totalled over £2m.

 

Between 1990 and 1999 at Oxford University, I founded and led the Hardware Compilation Research Group. The group was concerned with constructing software compilation technology to make it possible for the design and implementation of combined hardware and software systems to be accomplished by system-level programmers alone. A central aim was to remove any dependence on explicit hardware knowledge, so that the design process could be accomplished faster and more reliably, with commonly available skills, even against the commercial backdrop of rapidly increasing system complexity.

 

In 1990 I developed the ‘Handel’ method (named after the composer) of designing parallel software and wrote the first Handel compiler which compiled such programs directly into efficient parallel hardware for FPGA implementation. Today, those programs are expressed in the language 'Handel-C' which looks, syntactically, like the 'C' programming language. In fact, Handel-C owes much to the 'Occam' programming language and to Prof. Sir Tony Hoare's CSP algebra, on which both languages were based. The key innovation of the Handel-C language is that a programmer can easily control both the temporal and spatial aspects of the hardware implementation but without having to acquire any hardware knowledge.

 

I have over 25 years of technical consulting history alongside my academic career. I have consulted on a wide variety of topics, including computer-based training, parallel processing, office automation, interactive computer graphics, home and arcade video games, computer-based animation for television, video-disc based educational and simulation systems, bio-medical applications and display technology.
