Ian Page : D.Phil. Research Students

Current Research Students.

  • Dominic Camus. Corpus Christi College. EPSRC studentship. Started 1995. Working on place and route algorithms for hardware. Previously obtained a B.Sc. in mathematics at Oxford and an M.Sc. on the OUCL course with a project on automatic layout of circuit diagrams from unstructured netlists.
  • Karel Hruda. Balliol College. EPSRC studentship. Started 1996. Previously obtained a B.A. on the Oxford Mathematics and Computation Course.
  • Christian Keane. Jesus College. EPSRC studentship. Started 1996. Previously obtained a degree in Computing at Cambridge. Now mainly supervised by Jon Saul.
  • Paul Metzgen. EPSRC studentship. Starting 1996. Working on hardware/software co-design and placement/routing issues. Now supervised by Jon Saul.

Past Research Students.

  • Maurice Inuani. Rhodes Scholar. Magdalen College. Started 1993. Working on applications of hardware compilation and on the Oxford FPGA architecture. Previously obtained a B.A. on the Oxford Engineering and Computation Course and worked on BDD optimisation of digital logic for his final year project. Co-supervised with Jon Saul. Awarded D.Phil 1998.
  • Ken Lomax. EPSRC studentship. St. Hugh's College. Started in 1993 working on techniques and algorithms for singing synthesis. Previously worked for Roland U.K. and took the OUCL M.Sc. course. Jointly supervised with John Coleman (Head of Phonetics Department) from Michaelmas '94. He has also had an internship with Xavier Rodet at IRCAM in Paris. He obtained his D.Phil in summer 1997. His home page has examples of his synthesiser working and pointers to some of the media coverage his work has received.
  • Randal Pinkett. Rhodes Scholar at Keble College. M.Sc. (by research) awarded May 1996. Worked on applications of hardware compilation, particularly in the area of speech processing. Previously took a degree in Electrical Engineering at Rutgers and worked in the speech coding group at Bell Labs.
  • Chris Patmore. St. Hugh's College. D.Phil awarded 1995. Worked on graphics rendering techniques for complex real-world images, primarily trees and clouds. Has also produced a 3-D animation of a Canaletto painting, and has worked on virtual reality applications for Division Ltd. Previously obtained a B.A. on the Oxford Mathematics and Computation course.
  • Robin Watts. EPSRC studentship. St. Catherine's College. Started 1993. Worked on applications of hardware compilation and hardware optimisation techniques. Previously took the Oxford Mathematics and Computation B.A. and worked on parametrised processors. Sponsored by ARM Ltd. under the EPSRC CASE award scheme. Went off to work for lots of money before he finished his DPhil!
  • Matt Newman. EPSRC studentship. St. John's College. Started 1992. Working on geometrical aspects of hardware compilation; primarily on using information derived from the high-level user program to drive the placement process. Previously worked on mathematical topology in the Mathematics Institute. Another one that left to make lots of money without finishing his DPhil.
  • Phil Richards. EPSRC studentship. St. Hugh's College. Started 1988. Working on Greengard-style decomposition techniques for the n-body problem, particularly in the context of molecular modelling. Strong subsidiary interest in graphics rendering. Previously obtained a B.A. on the Oxford Mathematics and Computation course. Phil left before finishing his DPhil although not with the intention of making lots of money. He's probably done it however - they mostly seem to!
  • Andrew Leaver. St. Cross College. D.Phil. awarded 1995. Worked on routing networks for FPGAs and the general place/route problem. Previously took a B.Sc in Electronics at Southampton. Now working for Altera in San Jose.
  • Ken Wood. D.Phil. awarded 1992. Lincoln College. Jointly supervised with Prof. C.A.R. Hoare. Worked on software engineering techniques in the context of parallel hardware simulation. Previously obtained a B.A. in Applied Maths at Harvard and took the Oxford Computation M.Sc. course.
  • Phil Winder. D.Phil. awarded 1992. St. Hugh's College. Computational models of attention focussing and texture segregation algorithms. Previously took a B.A. in Physics at Cambridge and then the Oxford Computation M.Sc. course and worked on SIMD parallel computation of graphics/vision algorithms.
  • Christine Lee. Marshall Scholar. M.Sc. by research, awarded 1991. Green College. Worked on computer-based models of melanoma with Dr. Jonathan Stretch of the Radcliffe Infirmary in Oxford who pioneered the technique of large-area sectioning of melanomas, and IBM UK Scientific Centre, Winchester who scanned and image-processed the sections.
  • Alastaire Horn. D.Phil. awarded 1990. Worcester and Wolfson Colleges. Fractal representations of objects and associated graphics rendering techniques. Previously obtained a B.A. on the Oxford Physics course.
  • Wayne Luk. D.Phil. awarded 1989. St. Edmund Hall and Oriel College. Declarative frameworks for hardware design; research assistant to Geraint Jones on his Ruby project while working for his D.Phil. Previously obtained a B.A. on the Oxford Engineering Science course. Subsequently became Croucher Fellow, Rank Xerox Research Fellow and then senior researcher on the OMI/HORN project. Now lecturing at Imperial College, University of London. Academic visitor to the Computing Laboratory and still a strong contributor to our Hardware Compilation Group.
  • Kavi Arya. D.Phil. awarded 1988. St. Cross College. Computer animation in a functional programming framework. Now a freelance writer and programmer in Bombay. Previously took a B.Sc. in Computer Science at Imperial College and an M.Sc in Computation at Oxford. Kavi is now running the Chetana Publishing and Indian Cultural Centre.
  • Theoharis Theoharis. D.Phil. awarded 1988. Wolfson College. SIMD and MIMD parallel algorithms in computer graphics, particularly polygon rendering. Subsequently became Junior Research Fellow at St. Catherine's College, Cambridge. Now lecturing at Athens University. Previously took a B.Sc. in Computer Science from Queen Mary College, University of London, and an M.Sc. in Computation from Oxford.
